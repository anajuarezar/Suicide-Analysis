Understanding World Suicide Patterns


Suicide is a serious global public health issue. Globally, around 700,000 to 800,000 people die by suicide every year and it is among the leading causes of death worldwide, having a higher death count than malaria, HIV/AIDS, breast cancer, or war and homicide.
More than one in every 100 deaths (1.3%) in 2019 were the result of suicide according to the World Health Organization.




Social, psychological, cultural and other factors can interact, leading a person to present suicidal behaviour. But one of the main problems is the stigma that is connected to mental disorders and suicide. This means people won't talk about how they are feeling and won't look for help. With timely interventions, suicide is preventable, but usually mental health and suicide are on the bottom of the list as a priority for governments and policy-makers.


Our Project

Aim of the study

Why are we doing this?

As part of a World Suicide Patterns Proyect, this proposal aims to understand better the changes in suicide rates with a Machine Learning model, while using world development indicators to gather conclusions, in order to determine the most effective solutions.
One message prevails: suicide is preventable. Due to the complex nature of this phenomenon, suicide prevention requires collaboration, and different integrated and comprehensive approaches and studies. While we can identify most of the risk factors that lead to suicide, we cannot accurately predict them.
This is where machine learning comes in handy, using several models with different levels of accuracy, we can identify treatable and modifiable risks and protective factors. And so, this type of assessment is a new approach to an ever-growing problem, an effort to better address the problem and prevent more suicides.
